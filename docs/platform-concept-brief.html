Project Context Concept Brief · v1

A multi-tenant vertical SaaS for very small businesses.

From a portfolio of bespoke client websites toward a configurable platform with an opinionated ops backend and an eventual B2B network.

01 — Origin

The pattern that triggered the idea

Across recent client work, the same scaffolding keeps reappearing: brand-presence website, common content types, an intake form with conditional logic, and — for some — commerce or inventory tied to Square. Different industries, near-identical underlying needs.

Client landscape

Product
Tangible producers
  • Farm-to-table operations
  • Etsy-style creators
Hybrid
Product + service
  • Screen printers
  • Small carpentry / contractors
  • Bars & restaurants
  • Food trucks
Service
Pure service providers
  • Marketing & graphic design
  • Web design
  • Videography & photography

Current delivery model

Today these clients receive WordPress, Squarespace, or Shopify builds — setup, customization, and production push, billed per engagement. Commerce is occasionally a factor; Square integration is a recurring requirement for the food/farm cohort, including live inventory sync.

02 — The existing engine

The MERN app already built for the agency

A working, single-tenant application that runs the operator's digital advertising agency. Its structure is essentially the proposed platform in miniature — which is what made the multi-tenant thesis visible in the first place.

Public surface

  • Project portfolio & company information
  • Conditional lead-management intake form — onboards everyone from "I don't know what I need" to "here is my exact scope and budget"

Operations backend

  • Clients → Projects → Tasks → Time entries → Invoicing
  • Service-discipline definitions and per-customer rate management
  • Block-time scheduling for todo navigation and quick time entry
  • Proposal creation
  • Team management with discipline definitions and rates
  • Domain assignment + Auth0 for admin access
03 — Thesis

The platform concept

One-line framing

A business-type-aware, multi-tenant web platform: businesses sign up, declare their type, toggle feature sets, configure simply, point a domain, populate content — and over time, transact with one another inside the network.

Configuration model

The platform knows what kind of business each tenant is. A farmer's tenant ships with inventory + CSA + seasonal content patterns. A screen printer's tenant ships with quote intake + proof approval + portfolio gallery. The opinionation per vertical is the product — not a blank canvas with plugins.

Long-term B2B layer

Once enough tenants exist across complementary verticals, the network becomes the differentiator: a restaurant can source a farmer, a marketing outfit can assemble vendors and providers for a live event, estimates and transactions can flow inside the platform rather than outside it.

Where this is genuinely promising

✓

Vertical opinionation is the wedge. Squarespace and WordPress are generic; this is opinionated by business type. That's a real differentiator against entrenched horizontal players.

✓

The ops backend is the moat. Website builders give a site; this gives clients/projects/tasks/invoicing wired in. Small businesses currently cobble this from five SaaS tools or spreadsheets.

✓

The B2B network is a credible long-term story — useful for narrative and direction, even before it's a built feature.

Where to be cautious

!

"Configurable for everyone" is the classic platform trap. The shared core across verticals is closer to 40% than 80%. Start narrow — likely farmers + food trucks (shared Square integration, natural B2B pair) — before expanding.

!

Two-sided marketplaces are hard. Cold-start problems are real. Treat B2B as phase-3, architect for it, don't build it early.

!

Multi-tenancy in MERN is non-trivial. Data isolation, custom-domain SSL, per-tenant feature flags, theming — a meaningful re-architecture from the single-tenant agency app.

!

Auth0 will get expensive at scale. Fine for now; plan an exit path.

04 — Strategy

The staged go-to-market

Rather than chase a self-serve SaaS launch, the chosen path is a productized service that accretes into a platform — using paid client work as the R&D budget. Each engagement generalizes one more piece of the system.

01Now

Pitch one-off builds personally

Sell to known prospects — bars, food trucks, restaurants, screen printers — using self-built templates. Deliver as standalone properties; the platform thesis is internal, not customer-facing.

02Build

Build with long-term data models in mind

Every per-client engagement uses the multi-tenant data model under the hood, even though the builds are sold as one-offs. The discipline: every feature request gets tested against "would two or more business types use this?"

03Learn

Observe real usage

With a few varied businesses live, watch habits and friction. Improvements flow back into the core platform instead of into one-off forks.

04Bridge

Migrate the operator's own business

Operator becomes a tenant on the new system — eating own dog food on a multi-tenant version that has already been stress-tested on paying clients.

05Network

Backdoor the B2B layer

With a family of trusted clients across complementary verticals, introduce connections manually first ("my food truck client needs produce — meet my farmer client"). Build the marketplace UI only after the connection patterns are understood by hand.

05 — Discipline

What this strategy requires of the operator

→

A written rule for core vs. one-off. Every client request answers: config value or code change? Two-or-more-verticals → core. Single-client desire → tenant config or declined.

→

Deliberate client mix in phase 1. Coherent verticals (food/Square cluster + creator/intake cluster). Politely defer wildly different verticals — weddings, B2B consulting — until the model is mature.

→

Pricing that reflects R&D. Generalization takes longer than hardcoding. Price engagements above pure deliverable cost, or accept that the platform is a deliberate time investment.

→

Pre-set graduation criteria for phase 2. Sketch the trigger now — N tenants, stable feature set, recurring revenue threshold — so one-off mode doesn't become permanent.

→

Watch the bottleneck moment. Around client 6–7, build capacity stalls platform progress. That's when to hire, package the build process, or slow intake — not when to push through.

06 — Architecture

Operative decisions taken so far

Engine
The existing single-tenant MERN agency app is the prototype, not the production codebase. The next version is multi-tenant from day one.
Tenant zero
Build multi-tenant scaffolding first, migrate the operator's own business onto it as tenant #1, then onboard external client as tenant #2 — flipped from the originally proposed order, for safer bug discovery and a live demo tenant.
Config vs. core
Config (per-tenant): branding, content, visible features, intake-form questions, rate cards, theme. Core (shared, evolves for all): data models, workflows, integrations (Square, Stripe, Auth0), admin patterns.
B2B marketplace
Architected for, not built in phase 1. Data model permits it; transaction flows, escrow, dispute resolution deferred until tenant density exists.
Initial verticals
Food/Square cluster (farmers, food trucks, restaurants, bars) plus creator/intake cluster (screen printers, small contractors). Diversification deferred.
Identity
Auth0 short-term. Exit path to Clerk, WorkOS, or self-hosted modeled before scale.
07 — Status

Where the concept stands today

The plan is grounded: a productized service with a platform backbone, funded by client work, gradually accreting into a multi-tenant system with an eventual B2B layer. Stage 1 — pitching and delivering one-off builds to known prospects using the new multi-tenant scaffolding — is the next concrete activity.

Open questions to revisit in subsequent discussions

Q · Timeline

How soon does the first external client build happen on the refactored multi-tenant version — weeks, or a longer rebuild period first?

Q · Multi-tenancy strategy

Shared DB with tenant_id, schema-per-tenant, or DB-per-tenant? Custom-domain SSL provisioning approach (Caddy or alternative)? Per-tenant feature-flag system?

Q · Config schema

What does the per-tenant configuration object look like in v1 — minimum fields to support the food and creator clusters without prematurely generalizing?

Q · Pricing model

Setup fee plus monthly? Equity slice? Recurring percentage? How does pricing acknowledge that early builds are also platform R&D?

Q · Graduation criteria

Specific markers that trigger the move from "I do every build" to "tenants can self-serve" — number of tenants, MRR, feature stability, or a combination?
